# Agentic-AI Liability — Minimum-Evidence Baseline for Underwriting

*Baseline version: `EP-ATTESTATION-BASELINE-v1` · 2026-07 · SPDX-License-Identifier: Apache-2.0*

> **What this document is — and is not.**
> This is a **proposed** minimum-evidence baseline: eight criteria (AB-1 … AB-8) an underwriter
> can demand from any insured that runs AI agents against money, records, or production systems,
> before crediting the insured's authorization control in pricing. The criteria are
> **mechanism-neutral** — they name properties of evidence, not products. Any format, any vendor,
> any in-house build that meets a criterion satisfies it. This document **grades nobody**: it
> names no AI vendors, no insureds, and no carriers, and it renders no opinion on any
> organization's control environment. Section 3 maps the criteria to one candidate
> implementation (EMILIA Protocol artifacts) and is explicitly labeled as such. Read the honesty
> block in section 4 before relying on anything here.

---

## 1. The underwriting problem

Premium credit needs a testable control. Cyber insurance found one: MFA. It is binary enough to
put on an application, cheap enough to verify at binding, and its absence is visible in the
loss record when a claim arrives. Agentic-AI liability has no equivalent yet. Carriers are being
asked to price the risk that an autonomous agent releases a payment, alters a vendor master
record, deletes a production resource, or exfiltrates data — and the application question that
would separate a good risk from a bad one does not exist in standard form. "Do you use AI
responsibly" is not a control. "Do you have human oversight" is not testable. What underwriting
needs is the same thing it has always needed: a control whose presence can be verified before
binding, whose operation leaves a record during the period, and whose record survives contact
with a claims dispute.

The record most insureds can produce today is the AI vendor's own activity log — and that is a
record produced by the party whose product caused the loss. At claims time its evidential value
is close to zero: it is generated after the action, by software the claimant's counsel will
correctly characterize as interested; it can typically be edited or regenerated by the operator;
and no third party can recompute its claims from the artifact itself. It is testimony, not
evidence. A baseline worth adopting has to demand records that are produced *before* execution,
bound to the *exact* action, and verifiable by the carrier's own tooling without trusting the
insured, the vendor, or the agent. The eight criteria below are that demand, written so a
carrier can put them on an application, test them at binding, and re-test them on the loss file.

## 2. Baseline criteria AB-1 … AB-8

Conventions used in every criterion:

- **"In-scope actions"** = the consequential action families the policy cares about (funds
  movement, credential and permission changes, record alteration, destructive infrastructure
  operations, data export) as scheduled between carrier and insured at binding.
- **"At binding"** = verification the underwriter (or a delegated assessor) performs on the
  submission. **"At claims time"** = verification performed on the loss file.
- Each criterion states **what a vendor activity log alone cannot satisfy** — the gap between
  the record most insureds have and the record the criterion demands.
- The criteria are cumulative: AB-1 through AB-5 describe the minimum record; AB-6 through AB-8
  describe how the record scales to hard actions, to the uncontrolled surface, and to the
  policy period.

### AB-1 — Pre-execution authorization bound to the exact action

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | For every in-scope action executed by or at the direction of an AI agent, the insured can produce an authorization record that was created **before** the action executed and is **bound to the exact action parameters** that executed — amount, counterparty, account, resource identifier, or equivalent material fields — such that the record for one action cannot be presented as authorization for a different one. |
| **Why it prices risk** | The dominant agentic loss mode is not "the agent acted with no approval anywhere" — it is "something was approved, and something else executed." Binding authorization to exact parameters before execution is the difference between a control that stops parameter drift (wrong amount, swapped payee, broader scope) and a workflow that merely decorates it. An insured that can show pre-execution exact-action binding has closed the loss mode a generic approval step leaves open. |
| **At binding** | Sample recent in-scope actions from the insured's environment; for each, demand the authorization record and agree its bound fields to the parameters of the action that actually executed. Confirm the binding is structural (the record contains or commits to the fields) rather than narrative (a ticket that describes the action in prose). |
| **At claims time** | For the loss event, demand the authorization record for the exact action that caused the loss. Either it exists and its bound fields match the executed parameters (the action was authorized as executed), or the fields diverge (drift the control should have refused), or no record exists (the action ran outside the control — see AB-7). Each of the three outcomes is directly dispositive for the file. |
| **Vendor log gap** | A vendor activity log records what the agent did, after it did it. It cannot show that a human bound themselves to those exact parameters *before* execution, and an after-the-fact narrative entry can be made to describe whatever executed. |

### AB-2 — Independent verifiability of the authorization record

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | The authorization record verifies using the carrier's own tooling — or any third party's — against a published format, offline, without querying the insured's systems, the vendor's API, or any party with an interest in the outcome. Verification is cryptographic (signature over the record's content by a key attributable to the authorizer under the insured's stated key-management practice), not a lookup in the producer's database. |
| **Why it prices risk** | A record only prices risk if it still means something when the producer is the adverse party. At claims time the insured, the vendor, and the claimant may all have incentives to shade the record. A record the carrier can recompute from the artifact alone removes the producer from the trust path — the difference between evidence the file can rest on and an exhibit that needs a sponsoring witness. |
| **At binding** | Take a sample of authorization records and verify them with tooling the insured did not supply configuration for at verification time (published format, carrier- or assessor-operated verifier, keys pinned from the insured's declared registry). A record that verifies only inside the insured's or vendor's own console fails this criterion. |
| **At claims time** | Re-verify the loss-event record independently. Confirm the signing key was within its declared validity window and not revoked at the time of authorization, per the insured's key registry as declared at binding. |
| **Vendor log gap** | Vendor log entries are typically unsigned rows in the vendor's datastore, verifiable only by asking the vendor. Even where the vendor signs its export, the signature proves the *vendor* produced the export — not that an accountable human authorized the action. The signature the criterion demands is the authorizer's, not the platform's. |

### AB-3 — Tamper-evident, append-only decision record

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | Every authorization decision on in-scope actions — allowed **and** refused — is appended to a record in which the removal, insertion, or alteration of any entry is detectable by a third party from the record itself (for example, via a verifiable hash chain or an equivalent integrity structure). The insured can state, and demonstrate, what the integrity check reports over any requested window. |
| **Why it prices risk** | An editable record is worth what the editor wants it to be worth. Tamper-evidence converts "the insured says nothing was deleted" into "deletion is detectable, and here is the check." It also protects the *insured*: an intact chain over the loss window is affirmative evidence that the record presented is the record that was kept, which shortens disputes instead of extending them. |
| **At binding** | Run (or observe the insured run) the integrity verification over a recent window; confirm it completes and reports either intact or the exact position and nature of a break. Confirm decisions are recorded at decision time, not reconstructed at export time. |
| **At claims time** | Verify integrity over the window surrounding the loss event before relying on any entry in it. A break in the chain near the event is itself a material fact for the file. |
| **Vendor log gap** | Vendor activity logs are, as a rule, mutable by the operator and by the vendor, and offer no third-party-checkable integrity structure. "Our logs are immutable" asserted by the party holding root on the log store is a policy statement, not a property the carrier can test. |

### AB-4 — Deny-by-default enforcement with observable refusals

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | The control refuses in-scope actions that arrive without sufficient authorization — and those refusals are visible in the decision record with machine-readable reasons. The insured can produce, for any period, the count and rate of refusals by reason. A control under which no refusal has ever occurred is treated as untested, not as flawless. |
| **Why it prices risk** | A preventive control that never demonstrably prevents anything is unfalsifiable, and unfalsifiable controls cannot earn premium credit. Refusal events are the control's loss-run: they show the control in the path of real traffic, making real decisions, with a decision distribution the underwriter can read the way a cyber underwriter reads blocked-intrusion telemetry. The refusal reasons also reveal *which* failure modes the environment actually produces (missing authorization vs. insufficient assurance vs. parameter drift). |
| **At binding** | Demand the refusal distribution for the trailing period: total in-scope decisions, refusals, and refusals by reason. Where feasible, test enforcement directly — submit or observe an unauthorized in-scope request and confirm it is refused and recorded. Enforcement demonstrated beats enforcement described. |
| **At claims time** | Examine refusals in the loss window. Refusals adjacent to the loss event (the control pushing back before an eventual bypass or approval) materially change the narrative of the file, in either direction. |
| **Vendor log gap** | Vendor logs record actions the agent took; they rarely record actions the platform *refused to take* on authorization grounds, and almost never with machine-readable refusal reasons attributable to a customer-controlled policy. Absence of refusals in a vendor log is silence, not evidence of control operation. |

### AB-5 — One-time use and replay defense

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | An authorization is consumable once: after the authorized action executes, re-presentation of the same authorization is refused and the refusal is recorded. The insured can produce the count of replay refusals for any period. |
| **Why it prices risk** | Without one-time consumption, a single legitimate approval becomes a standing capability — one authorized $50k payment becomes N payments, one approved deletion becomes a template for many. Replay is the cheapest amplification attack against any approval workflow, agentic or not, and agents make it cheaper by industrializing the re-presentation. A control with demonstrated replay defense caps the blast radius of every individual approval at one action. |
| **At binding** | Test directly where feasible: present a consumed authorization a second time and confirm refusal plus a recorded refusal event. Otherwise, demand the replay-refusal counts from the decision record and inspect a sample of the refusal entries. Inquire how consumption state is made durable across the insured's process boundaries — single-process replay defense on a multi-node deployment is a stated limitation the underwriter should record. |
| **At claims time** | Where a loss involves repeated actions, check whether the repetitions each carried distinct authorizations or replayed one. Distinct authorizations point to an authorization-process failure; successful replays point to a consumption-defense failure; refused replays followed by execution anyway point to a bypass (AB-7). |
| **Vendor log gap** | Vendor logs have no concept of an authorization being spent — they record each execution as a fresh event, with nothing that distinguishes "separately authorized N times" from "authorized once, replayed N-1 times." |

### AB-6 — Escalated multi-party proof for designated hard actions

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | For a scheduled subset of in-scope actions designated *hard* (highest-severity: large funds movements, permission/credential grants, production-destructive operations — the schedule agreed at binding), authorization requires multiple distinct humans with distinct credentials, and the record proves it: each approver's authorization is independently verifiable per AB-2, and the record demonstrates distinct identities and distinct keys meeting a stated threshold. A workflow screenshot or a ticket showing two names does not meet this criterion; a per-approver cryptographic record does. |
| **Why it prices risk** | The two-person rule is underwriting's oldest fraud control, and its known failure mode is that "two people approved" usually reduces at trial to one system administrator's say-so about what a workflow UI displayed. A per-approver verifiable record makes the second person load-bearing: collusion or compromise must now capture two credentialed humans, not one workflow database. For the actions that generate tail losses, this is the difference between a severity cap the carrier can price and one it has to take on faith. |
| **At binding** | Agree the hard-action schedule to the policy's severity concerns. Sample hard-action records and verify each approver's authorization independently; confirm the identities and credentials are distinct and the threshold was met, from the record rather than from the workflow tool's summary. Inquire how approver credentials map to current personnel (roster discipline) — the mapping of keys to genuinely distinct, currently authorized humans is the insured's process and should be tested as such. |
| **At claims time** | If the loss event was a hard action, re-verify every approver's record independently. A hard action executed with fewer verifiable approvals than the schedule requires is a control breakdown the file must reflect. |
| **Vendor log gap** | A vendor log can record that a workflow reached an "approved" state; it cannot prove two distinct humans independently bound themselves to the exact action, because the log's account of who clicked what is exactly the single-database say-so the criterion exists to replace. |

### AB-7 — Coverage accounting: the uncontrolled surface is measured, not assumed zero

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | The insured can state which in-scope action families are routed through the control and produce, from the decision record, a measurable account of the *uncontrolled* surface: actions that executed as unguarded pass-throughs, authorizations granted with one-time consumption or another defense explicitly disabled, and every emergency-override (break-glass) use — each attributed, bounded, and recorded, not inferred. Emergency access that leaves no record fails this criterion outright. |
| **Why it prices risk** | Controls fail at their edges, and agentic systems multiply edges: new tools, new integrations, new action paths appear faster than manifests are updated. A control covering 60% of consequential actions is a materially different risk from one covering 98% — but only if the insured can measure the difference. Coverage accounting converts "we route everything through the control" (unverifiable) into a number that can be warranted, priced, and re-checked at renewal. Break-glass discipline is the sharpest signal in the set: an insured whose emergency path produces *stronger* records than its normal path has thought about the failure mode; an insured whose emergency path is "disable the control" has not. |
| **At binding** | Demand the coverage account for the trailing period: guarded decision counts by action family, unguarded pass-through counts and the action names involved, defense-bypass counts, and the complete break-glass record (who invoked, scope, time bound, stated reason). Reconcile the guarded families against the in-scope schedule; the delta is the uncontrolled surface being priced. |
| **At claims time** | The first claims question under this baseline is: *was the loss action inside or outside the control's coverage?* The coverage account answers it from the record. A loss on a scheduled-but-unrouted family, or through an unrecorded emergency path, is a different file — and potentially a different coverage outcome — than a loss the control adjudicated. |
| **Vendor log gap** | A vendor log shows activity on that vendor's platform only. It cannot see actions taken through other tools, direct consoles, or APIs — and so cannot bound the uncontrolled surface at all. The criterion demands the insured's own enforcement-point accounting precisely because no single vendor's telemetry can provide it. |

### AB-8 — Deterministic period attestation, recomputable by the carrier

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| **Criterion** | For any policy period, the insured can produce an attestation of control operation computed **purely from the decision record**: control in force with first/last decision timestamps, decision volumes by action family, refusal counts and reasons, replay refusals, hard-action and multi-party usage, the AB-7 exception account, and the integrity status of the underlying record. The attestation is deterministic — the same record and parameters produce the same document — so the carrier can recompute it from the exported record rather than accept it. Entries the computation cannot verify are excluded from every count and disclosed as warnings, never silently included. The document states on its face what it does and does not attest. |
| **Why it prices risk** | This is the loss-run analogue: the periodic, standardized, recomputable summary that lets an underwriter price a renewal, compare year over year, and compare insured against insured on the same fields. Determinism is what makes it an instrument rather than a narrative — a report that cannot be recomputed is a brochure. The exclude-and-disclose rule matters equally: an attestation that quietly counts records it could not verify overstates the control in exactly the situations (partial corruption, partial migration, partial tampering) where overstatement is most dangerous. |
| **At binding** | Demand the attestation for the trailing period together with the underlying record export; recompute the attestation (or have an assessor do so) and agree it to the presented document. Review the disclosed warnings: their character (a handful of malformed legacy entries vs. systematic exclusions) is itself underwriting information. |
| **At claims time** | Recompute the attestation over the loss window from the record in the file. Discrepancy between the insured's presented attestation and the carrier's recomputation is a material fact; agreement closes an entire category of dispute before it opens. |
| **Vendor log gap** | Vendor dashboards summarize vendor telemetry on the vendor's terms: non-deterministic, non-recomputable, silent about what was excluded, and scoped to one platform. None of the eight properties above can be established from a summary the carrier cannot recompute. |

## 3. One candidate implementation: EMILIA Protocol artifacts

The criteria above are deliberately mechanism-neutral, and this section does not change that:
**any** implementation meeting AB-1 … AB-8 qualifies, including an insured's in-house build.
What follows maps the criteria to the artifacts of one candidate — EMILIA Protocol's Gate
enforcement point — so a carrier evaluating the baseline can see at least one existence proof
that every criterion is implementable today, with repository-level citations an assessor can
check. EMILIA Protocol is the author of this baseline; treat this section with the skepticism
that fact deserves, and test the mapping rather than accepting it.

| Baseline criterion | EMILIA artifact | Where it lives / how to check |
|---|---|---|
| **AB-1** Pre-execution exact-action binding | Authorization **receipt**: Ed25519 over canonical JSON, issued before execution, bound to the exact action fields; execution-field binding refuses "signed X, executed Y" drift by canonical-hash comparison of declared required fields. | `packages/gate/index.js` (deny-by-default `check`/`guard`); `packages/gate/execution-binding.js`; per-action bound fields in `packages/gate/action-packs.js` |
| **AB-2** Independent verifiability | Offline verification of receipts and per-signer proofs against pinned issuer keys; **reliance packets** carry what was authorized and what executed for third-party recomputation. The JavaScript, Python, and Go same-team ports agree across 18 suites and 251 vectors. A separately authored Rust implementation is rebuilt from a pinned public source tree and passes the pinned 16-suite/164-vector clean-room bundle plus 359 hostile cases, re-run in CI on every change. This is external interoperability evidence; strict clean-room construction acceptance remains zero pending independent attestation. | `packages/verify/`; `packages/gate/reliance-packet.js`; CF-1 check "evidence verifies offline" in `packages/gate/cf1-conformance.js` |
| **AB-3** Tamper-evident decision record | Hash-chained **evidence log** over canonical JSON: every decision, allow or deny, appended; `verify()` reports intact or the exact sequence position and reason of a break; strict mode refuses to authorize what it cannot durably record. | `packages/gate/evidence.js` |
| **AB-4** Observable refusals | Machine-readable **refusal reasons** on every deny (`receipt_required`, `assurance_too_low`, `replay_refused`, `execution_binding_failed`, …), refused with an HTTP 428 Receipt-Required challenge; refusal distributions computed per period. | Reason emission in `packages/gate/index.js`; EG-1 refuse/allow harness in `packages/gate/eg1-conformance.js` |
| **AB-5** One-time use / replay defense | One-time **consumption** of receipt ids with `replay_refused` recorded on re-presentation; fleet-safe operation requires the deployer to supply a durable atomic backend (stated limitation, per AB-5's binding inquiry). | `packages/gate/store.js`; replay checks in `packages/gate/gate.test.js` |
| **AB-6** Multi-party proof for hard actions | **Quorum** (`EP-QUORUM-v1`): M-of-N distinct humans, distinct keys, threshold met, per-signer assertions bound to the exact action and independently verified — never a self-asserted tier field. Mapping keys to currently authorized humans remains the insured's roster discipline, exactly as AB-6 requires the carrier to test. | `packages/verify/quorum.js`; tier enforcement in `packages/gate/index.js`; fabricated-quorum refusals in `packages/gate/redteam.test.js` |
| **AB-7** Coverage accounting | The underwriter attestation's **exceptions** section counts unguarded pass-throughs (with action names) and replay-defense bypasses; the Art. 14 pack computes a coverage ratio; **break-glass** is itself a signed, scoped, time-bounded, attributed, single-use grant on the same evidence chain — no evidence entry, no override. | `packages/gate/reports/underwriter.js` (exceptions block); `packages/gate/reports/art14.js`; `packages/gate/breakglass.js` |
| **AB-8** Deterministic period attestation | `EP-GATE-UNDERWRITER-ATTESTATION-v1`: a pure function over the evidence log — same entries and options in, identical JSON out; unverifiable entries excluded from every count and surfaced as `integrity_warnings`; carries its honesty block (attests control operation only) inside the artifact; near-miss/remediation narrative fields are emitted `null` for the broker, never machine-generated. | `packages/gate/reports/underwriter.js`; determinism and exclusion tests in `packages/gate/reports/underwriter.test.js` |

Two boundary notes on the mapping, so it is not over-read. First, formal analysis of the
protocol (TLA+/Alloy) covers the core state machine and quorum logic — it does not cover
WebAuthn binding or log checkpoints. Second, the protocol's Internet-Drafts are active
**individual** submissions on the IETF stream: drafts by their authors, not IETF-adopted or
endorsed standards. Neither fact is a criterion of the baseline; both belong in any honest
description of this candidate.

## 4. Honesty block — read before relying on this document

- **This is a proposed baseline, not an insurance standard.** It has no standing in any policy,
  application, or claim until a carrier adopts it, in whole or in part, on the carrier's own
  terms. Adoption, tailoring, thresholds, and every underwriting and claims conclusion belong
  to the carrier and its professionals.
- **EMILIA Protocol does not underwrite.** It is not an insurer, an auditor, or a regulator. It
  issues no policies, sets no rates, adjusts no claims, and renders no opinion on any insured.
  This document and the artifacts in section 3 exist to **support** underwriting and claims
  work; they conclude nothing.
- **What the evidence proves — and does not.** Verification under this baseline establishes
  signature validity, exact-action binding, assurance-tier crediting, and record integrity. It
  never establishes the business correctness or wisdom of an authorized action: an action can
  be perfectly authorized and still be a loss. Principal identities are as pinned by the
  deploying organization's key and roster practice, not independently proofed by any artifact
  here.
- **The criteria measure a control's record, not an organization's risk.** Meeting AB-1 … AB-8
  narrows specific, named loss modes; it does not make an insured a good risk, and this
  document grades no organization against the criteria.
- **Section 3 is one candidate, self-nominated.** The baseline's author has an interest in one
  of its possible implementations. That is disclosed here so the reader can weigh it; the
  criteria in section 2 are written so that the mapping in section 3 earns nothing the
  criteria's plain text does not demand of everyone equally.

*Change control: any edit that adds a criterion must add, in the same change, the "why it
prices risk," both verification rows, and the vendor-log-gap row — a criterion without all
four is not a valid `EP-ATTESTATION-BASELINE-v1` criterion.*
