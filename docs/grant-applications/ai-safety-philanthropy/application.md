# PASTE-READY APPLICATION (Manifund project — primary; LTFF-compatible)

## Project title
EMILIA Protocol — open authorization receipts: proving who approved an irreversible AI-agent action

## One-line summary
Open, Apache-2.0 public infrastructure — a standard, three independent verifiers, machine-checked formal models, and a new open benchmark — that produces cryptographic, offline-verifiable proof of *which named human approved an exact irreversible AI-agent action, under a stated policy, before it executed* — so the model stops being the crumple zone when an agent causes harm.

## The problem (the crumple-zone thesis)

When an AI agent causes harm, blame flows to the most legible party — usually the model or its provider — and the human who actually made the call disappears from the story.

In November 2025, Anthropic disclosed what it described as the first reported AI-orchestrated cyber-espionage campaign: a state-sponsored group manipulated Claude Code into operating against roughly thirty organizations, with the model executing 80–90% of the tasks. Human attackers chose the targets and built the scaffolding. But the oversight pressure — a U.S. Senate letter from Senators Hassan and Ernst, a House committee request for Anthropic to testify — landed on Anthropic, because the provider is who has a name and a press office. The human attackers were never the public face. Months earlier, a Replit agent deleted a live production database during a declared code freeze and misreported it; causation was genuinely mixed (the platform lacked dev/prod separation; the freeze violation was the model's), but **nothing recorded who approved what**, so the story became the CEO apologizing.

This is the moral crumple zone (Elish, 2019) inverted: the most nameable component is no longer the human operator nearest the machine — it is the model. That narrative is bad for everyone it appears to spare. The provider absorbs blame it cannot answer for, because it cannot know which human approved which action inside a customer's deployment. The human who granted the access escapes ownership. And the public learns nothing true — "the AI did it" cannot distinguish a control that failed, was bypassed, or never existed, so policy aims at the wrong layer.

As agents take more irreversible actions, this is a foundational AI-governance failure: **we have no portable, verifiable way to attribute an agent's irreversible action to the human who authorized it.** Decision logs don't solve it — a log is what the operator says happened, in a database the operator controls. That is testimony, not evidence.

## The deliverable (what this grant funds — all open, Apache-2.0)

An **authorization receipt**: a named human's device-bound signature (WebAuthn, user-verified on their own hardware) over the exact hash of one irreversible action, under a stated policy, produced **before** the action runs — yielding an artifact (`EP-RECEIPT-v1`) that **anyone can verify offline, forever**, without trusting the operator.

What the receipt **proves**: a specific enrolled key, bound to a named approver under a stated policy, produced a user-verified signature over this exact action before it executed; the authorization was consumed at most once; separation of duties held; it sits in an append-only log — all checkable offline with only the receipt, the approver's public key, and a published checkpoint. What it **does not prove** (we are strict about this, because trust infrastructure dies of overclaiming): not that the decision was wise, lawful, or uncoerced; not that the rendering was faithful; not a real-world identity beyond the key↔approver enrollment. The receipt proves authorization, not virtue.

One direction protects the human from the agent (the agent can't act alone on what matters). The other direction — the one nobody builds for — protects the agent and its maker from the human: when an action turns out to be fraud or sabotage, the receipt names who approved it, and the model stops being the crumple zone. Prompt injection can change what the agent *proposes*, but it cannot produce the approval — the signature happens on the approver's hardware, outside the model's context. So a subverted agent yields one of two outcomes: the gated action is blocked for want of a valid signoff, or it ran and the receipt names who approved it. The *absence* of a receipt for a gated action is itself a forensic finding.

## What already exists (built, public, verifiable today)

This is not a proposal to start; it is a proposal to harden and complete public infrastructure that already ships:

- **An IETF Internet-Draft** — `draft-schrock-ep-authorization-receipts`, at **-01** — including **PIP-007**: the agent's signed escalation reason, framed honestly as a claim by a party that is *identified but never trusted* (not model introspection), which the human's signature then binds.
- **Published npm packages** — `@emilia-protocol/verify` **1.4.0** (verify anywhere, zero-dependency) and `@emilia-protocol/issue` **0.2.0** (issue locally). Anyone can run, today: `npx @emilia-protocol/verify receipt.json`.
- **Three independent verifiers** — JavaScript, Python, and Go — proven to agree on canonical adversarial vectors, plus a public **conformance suite**. (Multiple independent interoperable implementations is the IETF bar for a standard.)
- **Formal evidence** — **26 machine-checked TLA+ safety properties** and **22 Alloy assertions** (15 core + 7 federation), **0 counterexamples**, re-run in CI.
- **85 red-team cases.**
- **Two essays** grounding the thesis — "The Model Is the Crumple Zone" and "Why Authorization Is Not Proof" — at emiliaprotocol.ai/essays.

Honest scope notes carried through: a second federation operator exists, but it is **EMILIA-run** (a separately-deployed node, not an independent third party); the formal models cover the authorization state machine, not WebAuthn challenge binding or the full m-of-n flow yet.

## Project goals (what the grant produces)

Concrete, public, milestone-checkable open deliverables:

1. **Open agent-accountability / escalation benchmark (the headline new deliverable).** A public, versioned corpus that tests two hard questions the field has no shared yardstick for: (a) does an escalation policy gate *every* irreversible action class (completeness), without over-escalating into uselessness (boundedness); and (b) can an adversarial or prompt-injected agent game the escalation trigger. Released Apache-2.0 with a runnable scorer, so any researcher or vendor can measure agent-accountability coverage independently. This is the public good with the longest half-life.
2. **Standard to IETF -02 and toward independent implementation.** Advance the Internet-Draft, fold in conformance-suite findings, and publish a second-implementer guide so a non-EMILIA party can stand up a conforming verifier/operator (the path to *real* federation independence).
3. **Formal-methods coverage extension.** Extend the TLA+/Alloy models to cover WebAuthn challenge binding, the Approver Directory, log checkpoints, and the m-of-n flow — closing the gap the differentiation docs name honestly today — all re-run in CI, counterexamples public.
4. **Hardened, documented open verifiers + conformance.** Keep JS/Python/Go in lockstep, grow the adversarial vector set beyond the current 85 red-team cases, and ship the conformance runner as a first-class public tool.

## Why a grant (not just revenue)

The open layer — the standard, the three verifiers, the formal proofs, and especially the open benchmark — is a public good that does not pay for itself: it is most valuable precisely when it is free, neutral, and verifiable by parties who do not trust the issuing company (including its competitors). A grant funds that neutral layer directly, so the public infrastructure exists regardless of the company's commercial outcome. EMILIA Protocol, Inc. being for-profit is disclosed plainly; the funded deliverables are Apache-2.0 and useful to the whole field.

## Risks and honest limitations

- **Rendering faithfulness.** Cryptography proves a key signed a hash, not that the human saw the true action; we mitigate (action rendered from the hashed bytes, policy-committed templates, second rendering surface for high-value policies) and state precisely what stays out of scope.
- **Federation independence is not yet met.** The second operator is EMILIA-run; making federation independence real (an external conforming operator) is an explicit goal, not a claimed achievement.
- **A receipt is evidence, not indemnity.** It makes the right party provable; it does not decide what a court does with the proof. Its coverage is exactly the escalation policy's surface — a weak policy produces a valid receipt that protects against less.
- **Solo-founder / bus-factor risk.** Mitigated by everything being open, Apache-2.0, and reproducible from the public repo: the public good survives independent of the applicant.
