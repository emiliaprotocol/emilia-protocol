Neutral technical reference · European digital sovereignty · July 2026

Digital sovereignty includes evidence Europe can verify itself.

Control over infrastructure is incomplete when evidence of a consequential AI action remains inside the operator's dashboard. EMILIA provides an open, self-hostable way to retain action-authorization evidence that a European institution can verify against trust inputs it controls.

The narrow control

A deployer may require an enrolled person, or a policy-required quorum, to authorize the exact material action before an executor proceeds. The resulting receipt is portable and can be checked offline without calling the issuing service.

A valid receipt establishes that a relying-party-pinned key signed the exact action under a stated policy context. It does not, by itself, establish legal identity, comprehension, competence, correctness, lawfulness, or full regulatory compliance.

Why this matters for European institutions

Institution-held trust

The assessor chooses and retains the approver, policy, log, revocation, and verifier trust inputs. The operator's assertion is not the root of verification.

Cross-boundary evidence

The same artifact can travel from provider to deployer, auditor, insurer, court, or market-surveillance authority without requiring the original service to remain online.

Open implementation

Specifications, code, vectors, and named formal-model claims are public and Apache-2.0. The protocol texts are active individual IETF Internet-Drafts, not adopted standards.

Assessment questions the evidence should survive

  • Was the signer key pinned outside the artifact?
  • Did the signature cover the exact material action fields?
  • Were initiator exclusion and distinct-human quorum enforced where required?
  • Do mutation, replay, stale authority, policy mismatch, and unavailable state refuse?
  • Can an outside party reproduce the result from the retained export?
