// The Problem
Trust Under Uncertainty
Agents, plugins, and marketplace apps make decisions at machine speed. But trust is still guesswork — star ratings, download counts, brand recognition. None of it is verifiable, contextual, or challengeable.
⚠

No Portable Trust

A merchant's trust on Amazon doesn't transfer to Shopify. A plugin's safety on npm doesn't transfer to MCP. Every ecosystem starts from zero.

🔒

No Context

A plugin safe for read-only access is not safe for code execution. A merchant trusted for electronics may fail at furniture. Generic trust is dangerous trust.

🔎

No Due Process

When trust goes wrong, there's no way to challenge it. EP's trust evaluation and appeals are open and auditable — every weight published, every decision explainable, every harm reversible.

// How It Works
Receipts, Not Reviews
Every review system has failed because the platform hosting reviews profits from the businesses being reviewed. EMILIA is different. The buying agent submits a receipt about the seller — and then uses that same trust profile to decide where to transact next. You rate what you eat. Lie, and you eat bad food again. The submitter and the consumer of the trust data are the same entity. This creates natural incentive alignment that no review system has ever achieved.
1
Entity Registers
Agents, merchants, and service providers register on the protocol. Each gets a permanent entity ID and API key.
2
Transactions Happen
Commerce flows through existing protocols — UCP, ACP, A2A, MCP. EP doesn't mediate the transaction. It evaluates whether the counterparty or software should be trusted next time.
3
Receipts Are Submitted
The buying agent submits a receipt with transaction_ref, behavioral outcome (completed/retried/abandoned/disputed), optional context keys (category, geo, value band), and structured claims. Three-factor weighted: submitter credibility × time decay × graph health.
4
Trust Recomputes
The trust profile recomputes from rolling receipts. Behavioral-first trust evaluation with effective-evidence dampening. Open, auditable protocol logic. No one can buy their trust.
// The Three Phases of Truth
From Self-Reported to
Protocol-Observed
PHASE 1
LIVE

Behavioral Truth

Buying agents submit receipts with behavioral outcomes as primary signal (40% weight). Did they come back? Switch? Abandon? Receipts from unestablished entities carry only 0.1x weight. Three-factor weighting: submitter credibility × time decay × graph health.

PHASE 2
IN DEV

Evidence-Backed Truth

Agents submit structured claims + evidence: "delivered 3 days late" with tracking ID, "price overcharged 5%" with payment ref. The engine computes the trust profile from facts. Counter-receipts within 48hrs. Conflicting claims enter disputed state — the dispute rate itself becomes a trust signal.

PHASE 3
ROADMAP

Protocol-Observed Truth

EP listens to MCP/A2A/UCP protocol events and auto-generates receipts. Decentralized verification oracles (with their own EP trust profiles — bad oracle gets downgraded) confirm claims against carrier APIs and payment processors. Zero-knowledge trust proofs for privacy.

THREE-FACTOR RECEIPT WEIGHTING

Every receipt carries a weight computed from three factors:

receipt_weight = submitter_credibility × time_decay × graph_health
SubmitterEstablished entity with high trust credibility carries close to full weight. Unestablished entity = 0.1x regardless of self-asserted quality. Time decay90-day half-life. Recent receipts matter more. Bad history fades. Recovery is rewarded. Graph healthClosed loops = 0.4x. Thin graphs = 0.5x. Clusters = 0.1x and blocked.
EFFECTIVE EVIDENCE DAMPENING

Trust outputs are dampened based on effective evidence (sum of weighted receipts), not raw receipt count. 5 perfect receipts from throwaway accounts = 0.5 effective evidence → trust barely moves above baseline. Fake volume does not create real confidence.

WHY LYING HURTS THE LIAR

You rate what you eat. Lie, and you eat bad food again.

The buying agent submits receipts AND uses those same profiles to route. Falsely endorse a bad merchant? Your own routing sends you back to that same bad counterparty. Structurally aligned. Bilateral attestations in Phase 2 make it even harder to game.

CONFIDENCE — TWO DISTINCT CONCEPTS

Historical establishment — has this entity ever built enough credible history? Permanent once achieved.
Current confidence — how much should you trust this profile right now? Computed from rolling-window effective evidence.

PENDINGEffective evidence = 0 INSUFFICIENTEffective evidence < 1.0 — receipts exist but carry very low weight PROVISIONALEffective evidence 1.0–4.9 — building credible history EMERGINGEffective evidence 5.0–19.9 — profile is meaningful CONFIDENTEffective evidence ≥ 20.0 — high confidence, broad evidence

An entity can be historically established but have declining current confidence. The API exposes both as separate objects.

// What No Other Trust System Does
Profiles, Not Scores.
Policies, Not Thresholds.
FICO gives you one number. EP gives you a multi-dimensional trust profile evaluated against configurable policies in context. Because a single number is naive — and trust is not generic.
TRUST PROFILES

Multi-Dimensional Trust

Behavioral rates (completion, retry, abandon, dispute), per-signal breakdowns, consistency, anomaly alerts. Agents use the profile, not a headline number.

TRUST POLICIES

Configurable Decisions

Four built-in policies: strict, standard, permissive, discovery. Or define your own as JSONB. Needs can require a trust policy — claim evaluation uses it, not a raw score threshold.

CONTEXT-AWARE

Trust Is Not Generic

A merchant can be excellent for electronics but weak for furniture. Evaluation accepts context — category, geo, value band — and filters receipts to matching context. Falls back to global only when context-specific data is sparse.

ANOMALY DETECTION

Velocity Matters

A trust profile at 70 that was 90 last week is alarming. EP tracks trust velocity — sudden drops trigger anomaly alerts. Agents can reject entities with severe anomalies even if the absolute number looks fine.

// Blockchain Verification
Receipts Are Immutable.
Provably.
EP receipts are stored in Supabase and cryptographically anchored on-chain via Merkle root publishing on Base (Coinbase L2). Not a crypto product — no tokens, no wallets. Just math.
🔗
Chained Receipts
Every receipt includes a SHA-256 hash of its contents plus the previous receipt's hash, forming a tamper-evident chain.
🌳
Merkle Anchoring
Batches of receipt hashes are compiled into a Merkle tree. The root is published to Base L2 at an estimated ~$0.60/month at launch scale (Base L2 gas costs vary).
✅
Proof Verification
Anyone can verify any receipt against the on-chain Merkle root. No trust in EMILIA required — verify the math yourself.

EP Must Never Make Trust
More Powerful Than Appeal.
This is the constitutional principle. If EP can influence routing, access, and conversion, then every negative trust effect must be explainable, challengeable, and reversible. Power without due process is dangerous.
Fake good reviews?
Three-factor weighting: submitter credibility × time decay × graph health. Unestablished = 0.1x. Effective evidence dampening kills Sybil attacks.
Sybil attack?
5 perfect receipts from throwaway accounts → trust barely moves above baseline. Graph analysis detects closed loops (0.4x), thin graphs (0.5x), clusters (0.1x + blocked).
Unfair downgrade?
Phase 2 adds due process: challenged → under review → resolved or reversed. Every trust record is append-only. Corrections supersede, never erase.
Trapped by bad history?
90-day half-life. Historical establishment is permanent. Current confidence reflects recent evidence. Recovery through sustained performance.
Gaming discovery?
Search and leaderboard rank by confidence or evidence depth, not just score. Filter by min_confidence. Compat score is a fallback sort key, not the primary trust language.
Spamming receipts?
Identity-aware write throttling — authenticated writes are rate-limited by API key + IP, not IP alone. Shared networks aren't punished. Different identities get separate quotas.

// Hard Questions, Straight Answers
FAQ
Who submits receipts? Can't merchants just submit good ones?
They don't. In EP, the purchasing agent submits the receipt about the merchant — not the other way around. Both parties can submit receipts about each other (bidirectional), but the entity being evaluated never controls their own receipts.

The purchaser has no incentive to inflate trust — if they vouch for a bad merchant, their own agent will route back and get burned. This creates natural incentive alignment that legacy trust systems lack: the submitter is also the buyer, and the buyer wants accurate data for their own future decisions.
Isn't the behavioral signal subjective? Doesn't that reintroduce the review problem?
No. Behavior in EP is not a star rating. It is a recorded outcome: completed, retried, switched, abandoned, or disputed. These are observable actions, not opinions.

completedTransaction finished, no retry needed retried_sameSame agent, same counterparty, new attempt (minor issue resolved) retried_differentSame task, switched to a different counterparty abandonedAgent stopped pursuing this task category entirely disputedAgent filed a formal dispute against the counterparty Behavioral signals carry the strongest weight in EP's trust model because they are the hardest to fake credibly. Did the agent come back? Did the transaction complete? Was there a dispute? These are verifiable outcomes, not self-reported ratings.

Trust in EP is never determined by one signal alone — it is computed from behavior, evidence, provenance, context, and policy.
How do you prevent fake entities from gaming the system?
Four layers:

Layer 1 — Registration friction. Rate-limited registration (max 5 entities/day per owner), email verification, optional micro-deposit staking.

Layer 2 — Graph analysis. Closed-loop detection (A scores B, B scores A), thin-graph flagging (many receipts but few unique submitters), velocity monitoring, cluster detection.

Layer 3 — Submitter credibility. Receipts from unestablished entities carry only 0.1x weight. To become established, an entity needs 5+ receipts from 3+ unique submitters. Creating 100 throwaway entities is useless — each carries 0.1x, and to get full weight, each throwaway would need its own network of established counterparties. The cost compounds quickly.

Layer 4 — Protocol design. No self-scoring. Rolling window of 200 receipts. New entity dampening. Bidirectional scoring means creating a fake buyer leaves a permanent, auditable trail. Every fake receipt is recorded forever — you can't clean up after a Sybil attack.
There are only 2 entities. Where's the traction?
EMILIA is a protocol, not a product. TCP/IP didn't have "users" — it had implementations. HTTPS didn't have "customers" — it had adoption.

What's live today: trust profiles, policy evaluation, context-aware receipts, install preflight for software, dispute lifecycle, human appeal, bilateral attestations, provenance tiers, conformance suite (JS + Python), 145+ tests across 7 suites, CI pipeline, MCP server with 14 tools, identity continuity spec (EP-IX), and blockchain verification. Registered entities already include software and MCP trust surfaces.

The traction metric for a protocol is adoption, not MAU. The MCP server means any AI agent or system can evaluate trust profiles, run install preflight, and file disputes with one config line. That's the distribution channel.
Does Amazon get evaluated, or the seller? Who exactly is an "entity"?
EP evaluates the direct economic counterparty — whoever took the money and made the promise.

Amazon.com (platform)Aggregate only — too big, shifts blame Seller "SofaCo-123"✅ Individual trust profile — took the money, made the promise FedEx / Amazon Logistics✅ Separate trust surface — made the delivery promise Your buying agent "Clara"✅ Yes — other agents verify if Clara is reliable Sofa arrives broken? SofaCo-123 gets as_described: false. FedEx gets delivered: true, on_time: true. Separate signals for separate entities. Unlike Yelp, which gives the restaurant one star because the delivery driver was late.
What's coming in Phase 2 and Phase 3?
Phase 2 — Evidence-Backed Truth (in development):
• Claims + evidence receipts: Instead of rating 0-100, agents submit "delivered 3 days late" with tracking ID, "overcharged 5%" with payment ref. The engine computes the trust profile from facts.
• Counter-receipts: Sellers can respond within 48 hours with their own evidence.
• Conflict resolution: When receipts conflict, both enter "disputed" state. 48-hour evidence window. If an oracle can verify a claim (e.g., carrier tracking confirms delivery time), it overrides. Otherwise both receipts are stored at 0.5x weight with a disputed flag. Entities with high dispute rates become inherently less trustworthy — the dispute rate itself is a signal.

Phase 3 — Protocol-Observed Truth (roadmap):
• Protocol listeners: EP observes MCP/A2A/UCP events and auto-generates receipts — no manual submission needed.
• Decentralized oracles: DeliveryOracle (carrier APIs), PaymentOracle (Stripe/AP2), ListingOracle (IPFS snapshots). Each oracle has its own EP trust profile — bad oracle gets trust-downgraded. Trust is recursive all the way down.
• ZK trust proofs: Prove "I meet this trust policy with sufficient evidence" without revealing any transaction details. Privacy-preserving trust.
• Cross-chain anchoring: Ethereum L1, Arbitrum, beyond Base L2.
What if Amazon or Shopify builds their own trust system?
They will — and that's fine. Amazon's trust data will only work on Amazon. Shopify's will only work on Shopify. EP works across all of them.

Machine-mediated systems are fundamentally cross-platform. A Shopify seller's Claude agent buying from an Amazon seller's Gemini agent — that transaction crosses three ecosystems. No single platform's trust system covers it. Only an open, protocol-level trust layer does.

Amazon internal trustWorks on Amazon only Shopify internal trustWorks on Shopify only EMILIA ProtocolWorks everywhere — protocol-level, chain-agnostic, open source Closed systems lose to open protocols. That's the bet.
Can an entity rebuild degraded trust?
Yes. Recovery is built into the protocol.

EP uses time-decay weighting: recent receipts carry more weight than old ones. Half-life is 90 days — a receipt from 90 days ago carries 0.5x weight, 180 days carries 0.25x, a year carries ~0.06x.

A merchant who was terrible 6 months ago but has been perfect since will see their trust profile improve as old receipts naturally decay. Bad history fades. Improvement is rewarded. Combined with the 200-receipt rolling window, this means no entity is permanently condemned — but recovery requires sustained good performance, not a single PR stunt.
Can an entity just re-register under a new name to escape bad trust?
They can — but it costs them everything they built.

EP does not solve identity by itself. It solves trust evaluation given an identity. If an entity abandons its ID and registers a new one, it starts over: pending confidence, zero effective evidence, zero receipts, no established counterparties. Trust does not transfer automatically.

EP also has a coarse anti-abuse signal for same-origin re-registration: the server derives an owner_id from a hashed client IP and rate-limits registration to 5 per day per owner. That is not a full identity system, but it makes throwaway re-registration more visible and less free.

The deeper solution is the planned identity binding interface (EP-IX) — a future extension that would let entities cryptographically prove continuity across key rotations, migrations, or platform changes.

For now, the main deterrent is simple: once trust becomes valuable, abandoning an entity means abandoning all accumulated trust with it.
What if agents lie about receipts?
EP does not assume honesty. It makes lying expensive and bounded.

Four layers limit the impact of false receipts:

1. Provenance tiers. Not all receipts count equally. Self-attested receipts carry much less weight than bilateral-confirmed or oracle-verified receipts.

2. Bilateral confirmation. A counterparty can confirm or dispute a receipt. Unconfirmed receipts remain lower-weight. Confirmed receipts are upgraded.

3. Graph penalties. Reciprocal loops and collusive clusters are penalized heavily. Entities that only transact inside a tight ring do not accumulate trust efficiently.

4. Effective-evidence dampening. A few low-trust receipts do not move the system very far. Meaningful trust requires credible evidence over time from multiple counterparties.

In EP-native agent networks, there is also often a self-correcting incentive: the agent that submits trust evidence is frequently also a future consumer of that trust data. If it falsely endorses a bad counterparty, it increases the chance it will route back into that bad outcome later.

So yes — agents can lie. EP is designed so that lies carry limited weight, truth carries more weight, and large-scale deception becomes expensive to sustain.
What if a business isn't using AI agents?
EP works without agents. It is agent-ready, not agent-dependent.

Any system that can make an HTTP request can participate: Shopify webhooks can submit receipts when orders complete, payment processor callbacks can generate trust evidence from transaction events, CRM or procurement systems can evaluate trust profiles before approving vendors, and human operators can view trust profiles, file disputes, and submit appeals at /appeal.

The protocol is just an API and a receipt format. Whether the sender is an AI agent, a webhook, a workflow automation, or a human operator, the interface is the same.

What changes is not the protocol — it is the provenance and evidentiary strength of the receipt.
Who decides whether an appeal or dispute is upheld?
Human input can trigger review, but it does not directly rewrite trust.

EP separates three things:

Reports — someone says something is wrong.
Disputes — a specific challenge is filed against a receipt or trust state.
Adjudication — an operator or designated reviewer decides whether the challenge is upheld, reversed, dismissed, or superseded.

That means humans can escalate contested trust, but they do not directly overwrite it through popularity, outrage, or reviews.

The protocol is designed so that trust can be challenged and corrected without erasing history.
What if someone falsely claims succession to a trusted entity?
Continuity claims are challengeable. They do not become trust-bearing immediately.

When a continuity claim is filed, it enters a 7-day challenge window. During this window, the old entity controller, active dispute counterparties, bound hosts, and operators can all challenge the claim.

If unchallenged, the claim proceeds to decision. If challenged, it requires operator review. Immediate approval is only permitted for tightly-scoped automated rotations with dual-signature proof and no active disputes.

This prevents hostile succession — where an attacker temporarily compromises a domain or account and tries to inherit a high-trust entity's profile before anyone notices. See EP-IX for the full continuity governance design.
What happens if a company splits or merges?
Fission does not multiply trust.

When a principal splits into multiple successors, EP-IX enforces conservation rules: one primary successor inherits the main trust lineage, secondary successors receive dampened partial continuity, and the total transferred trust cannot exceed the original.

All successors inherit the dispute burden — unresolved disputes, sanctions, and warnings follow every branch. A principal cannot shed bad history by splitting.

Merger support (A + B → C) follows the same principle: disputes aggregate, sanctions carry forward, and conflicting trust signals are resolved by evidence weight.
