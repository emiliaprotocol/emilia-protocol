// How It Works
Receipts, Not Reviews
Every review system has failed because the platform hosting reviews profits from the businesses being reviewed. EMILIA is different. Agents don't write opinions — they record what happened. Did the delivery arrive on time? Was the product as described? Was the price honored? These are verifiable facts. You can't fake a delivery receipt.
1
Entity Registers
Agents, merchants, and service providers register on the protocol with their capabilities.
2
Transactions Happen
Commerce flows through existing protocols — UCP, ACP, A2A. EMILIA doesn't handle the transaction.
3
Receipts Are Submitted
After each transaction, a receipt records what was promised vs delivered. Cryptographically signed. Immutable.
4
Scores Update
The EMILIA Score recomputes from rolling receipts. The algorithm is open source. No one can buy it.

// Blockchain Verification
Receipts Are Immutable.
Provably.
EP receipts are stored in Supabase and cryptographically anchored on-chain via Merkle root publishing on Base (Coinbase L2). Not a crypto product — no tokens, no wallets. Just math.
🔗
Chained Receipts
Every receipt includes a SHA-256 hash of its contents plus the previous receipt's hash, forming a tamper-evident chain.
🌳
Merkle Anchoring
Batches of receipt hashes are compiled into a Merkle tree. The root is published to Base L2 at ~$0.60/month at launch scale.
✅
Proof Verification
Anyone can verify any receipt against the on-chain Merkle root. No trust in EMILIA required — verify the math yourself.

// Hard Questions, Straight Answers
FAQ
Who submits receipts? Can't merchants just submit good ones?
They don't. In EP, the purchasing agent submits the receipt about the merchant — not the other way around. Both parties can score each other (bidirectional), but the entity being scored never controls their own receipts.

The purchaser has no incentive to inflate scores — if they score a bad merchant highly, their own agent will route back and get burned. This creates natural incentive alignment that review systems lack: the scorer is also the buyer, and the buyer wants accurate data for their own future decisions.
Isn't "agent satisfaction" (10%) subjective? Doesn't that reintroduce the review problem?
Fair concern. That's why agent_satisfaction is measured by what the agent did, not what it felt.

Completed without retry → 95 Retried same entity → 75 Switched entity → 40 Abandoned → 15 FICO doesn't ask borrowers to rate their lender. It watches whether they pay. EP doesn't ask agents to rate merchants. It watches whether they come back. At 10% weight, even a fully gamed signal barely moves the score — the other 90% is cryptographic fact.
How do you prevent fake entities from gaming the system?
Three layers:

Layer 1 — Registration friction. Rate-limited registration (max 5 entities/day per owner), email verification, optional micro-deposit staking.

Layer 2 — Graph analysis. Closed-loop detection (A scores B, B scores A), thin-graph flagging (many receipts but few unique submitters), velocity monitoring, cluster detection.

Layer 3 — Protocol design. No self-scoring. Rolling window of 200 receipts. New entity dampening. Bidirectional scoring means creating a fake buyer leaves a permanent, auditable trail. Every fake receipt is recorded forever — you can't clean up after a Sybil attack.
There are only 2 entities. Where's the traction?
EMILIA is a protocol, not a product. TCP/IP didn't have "users" — it had implementations. HTTPS didn't have "customers" — it had adoption.

What's live today: 14 API endpoints, published scoring algorithm (Apache 2.0), MCP server, TypeScript + Python SDKs, blockchain verification layer, and a formal protocol specification. Rex (#1) and Ruby (#2) are live AI agents that will generate real transaction data as agent commerce goes live.

The traction metric for a protocol is adoption, not MAU. The MCP server means any AI agent can check trust scores with one config line. That's the distribution channel.
