Decision Process

From appeal to outcome

Every appeal follows the same structured process. No discretion over procedure — only over judgment.

1

Report filed

Anyone files a report via POST /api/disputes/report or the appeal page. No auth required. Report is logged with submitter fingerprint.

Immediate
2

Automated intake

EP classifies the report type (false_claim, manipulation, policy_violation, sybil_network, etc.), validates the target entity exists, and assigns to the operator queue. Rate limiting checks run.

<30 seconds
3

Operator assignment

An available operator with relevant specialization is notified. Conflict-of-interest check: operators cannot review appeals involving entities they've submitted receipts for.

<4 hours
4

Evidence review

Operator reviews the trust profile, receipt chain, dispute history, and any submitted evidence. They may request additional evidence with a 48h window. All evidence requests are logged.

<72h total SLA
5

Decision

Operator selects one of three outcomes, writes a reasoning statement (required, minimum 50 chars), and submits.

upheld reversed dismissed
6

Trust propagation

If reversed: graph_weight set to 0 for implicated receipts, trust scores recomputed for all affected entities. The reversal cascades through the receipt graph. If upheld or dismissed: no trust change, decision logged.

<60 seconds

Accountability

How operators are held accountable

EP doesn't trust operators. It measures them. Every decision feeds a public performance record that anyone can query.

Public performance scores

Every operator's decision history is queryable via GET /api/operators/:id. Reversal rate, median SLA, agreement with protocol baseline, and appeal volume are all public.

Immutable audit trail

Every operator action is appended to the audit log with before/after state capture. Decisions cannot be edited retroactively. The SHA-256 chain links each action to the previous.

SLA enforcement

The cron/expire job escalates overdue appeals at 72h. Escalated appeals show in the public queue and count against the assigned operator's SLA score.

Conflict of interest checks

Assignment logic checks receipt submission history. If an operator has ever submitted a receipt for the disputed entity, they are excluded from that appeal automatically — not by honor system.

Baseline comparison

EP computes what the algorithmic evaluator would have decided. Operators who consistently diverge from the baseline in ways that benefit specific entity types are flagged for review.

Operator appeals

Operators can be removed by governance vote if their public record shows systematic bias. The process is defined in GOVERNANCE.md. No single party controls removal.

Taxonomy

What operators decide on

EP defines nine report types. Operators must classify their decision against the specific violation type — not a general "bad actor" determination.

false_claim

Entity submitted receipts for interactions that did not occur, or materially misrepresented what occurred.

manipulation

Entity coordinated with other entities to inflate trust scores through reciprocal attestations or cluster submission.

policy_violation

Entity failed a policy evaluation but continued operating or misrepresented their policy status to counterparties.

sybil_network

Multiple entity IDs appear to be controlled by the same principal for the purpose of laundering trust history.

impersonation

Entity registered with identifiers that mislead counterparties into confusing it with a different, established entity.

trust_farming

Entity submitted high volumes of low-value receipts to artificially increase receipt count without corresponding behavioral evidence.

evidence_tampering

Entity submitted modified or fabricated evidence during a dispute proceeding, including altered timestamps or signatures.

abuse_of_process

Reporter filed frivolous or retaliatory reports with intent to damage a competitor's trust profile rather than raise a legitimate concern.

other

Report does not fit an existing category. Operator must provide an extended reasoning statement and request a taxonomy addition if warranted.

Questions

Operator FAQ

Can I be anonymous as an operator? +

You may use a pseudonym. Your decision record is public under whatever name you register. However, EP staff know your real identity for conflict-of-interest verification. Pseudonymous operators receive the same rights and accountability as identified ones.

What happens if I make a bad decision? +

It is recorded publicly and counted in your agreement rate metric. Decisions can be reopened by a second operator if new evidence emerges — but the original decision remains in the audit log. Systematic bad decisions trigger a governance review. One-off errors are expected and handled gracefully.

How many appeals should I expect? +

EP is in early deployment. Expect zero to a few per month initially. The protocol is designed to handle most trust evaluation algorithmically — operator review is reserved for contested cases where algorithmic output is appealed. Volume will grow with adoption.

Can I recuse myself from a case? +

Yes. If you identify a conflict the system didn't catch, you should recuse. Use POST /api/disputes/:id/recuse with your reason. Recusals are logged and visible. Unexplained recusals on high-profile cases are noted in your record.

Is this compensation or volunteer work? +

Currently volunteer. Founding operators are recognized in the protocol history and shape how the appeal system works in practice. If EP develops a revenue model, operator compensation will be the first funded activity. We will not add paid tiers that advantage paid operators over volunteer ones.

Can an operator be removed? +

Yes, via the governance process in GOVERNANCE.md. Removal requires evidence of systematic bias, conflict-of-interest violations, or SLA abandonment. No single party can remove an operator — removal requires a majority of active operators and EP protocol maintainers to concur. The removed operator's decision record remains public.
