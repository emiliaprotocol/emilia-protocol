EMILIA Protocol: Human-Oversight Profile for Autonomous and High-Risk AI
                                Systems
              draft-schrock-ep-human-oversight-profile-00

Abstract

   Governing instruments for high-risk and autonomous AI require a human
   to remain meaningfully in control of consequential actions, but none
   specifies an artifact that proves it, after the fact, to a third
   party, without trusting the operator.  This document profiles the
   EMILIA Protocol (EP) authorization receipt
   ([I-D.schrock-ep-authorization-receipts]) as that verifiable human-
   oversight evidence layer.  It defines how to apply the receipt
   primitive at authorization boundaries for both human-in-the-loop and
   human-on-the-loop control, a small set of OPTIONAL authorization-
   context conventions, and the relying-party rules that make "a human
   was meaningfully in control" a verifiable artifact rather than an
   operator-owned log entry.  It introduces no new cryptography.  The
   profile is scoped to evidence, not cognition: it proves the fact,
   scope, currency, and authority of human authorization; it does not
   prove the human understood, or that the decision was wise or lawful.
   It is a necessary, not sufficient, condition, and says so throughout.
   Primary applicability is civilian high-risk AI; defense and lethal-
   autonomy regimes are noted as further, secondary applicabilities.

1.  Introduction and Motivation

   Every governing instrument for high-risk and autonomous AI requires a
   human to remain meaningfully in control, yet none specifies an
   artifact that proves it to a third party without trusting the system
   operator.  The lead applicabilities for this profile are civilian:

   *  *EU AI Act Article 14* requires high-risk AI to be effectively
      overseen by natural persons who can interpret output, decline to
      act on it, and intervene or halt.  (The AI Act, Art. 2(3),
      excludes systems used exclusively for military, defense, or
      national-security purposes; treat Art. 14 as a _civilian_
      applicability.)

   *  *NIST AI Risk Management Framework* calls for documented,
      auditable human oversight.

   The same evidence gap recurs in further, secondary applicabilities --
   DoD Directive 3000.09's "appropriate levels of human judgment over
   the use of force," and the UN CCW debate on meaningful human control
   of lethal autonomous systems -- which are noted here only to show the
   profile's reach, not as its primary framing.  Across all of them the

   unsolved problem is the same: when an autonomous system acts, the
   record that a named human authorized that exact action -- at the
   right scope, currently, under the right authority -- is a log the
   operator controls and could forge, backfill, or rubber-stamp.  EP
   closes that gap; this profile makes the mapping explicit.

2.  Control Modes and the Authorization Boundary

   This profile does NOT require per-machine-cycle human approval
   (incompatible with machine tempo).  EP receipts are issued at
   _authorization boundaries_ -- the discrete points where a human
   grants, scopes, or renews autonomous authority:

   *  *in_the_loop* -- a human authorizes each discrete consequential
      action before it executes (one receipt per action; the system
      fails closed without it).

   *  *on_the_loop* -- a human authorizes a bounded engagement envelope
      (action class, target/effect set, geofence, time window) within
      which the system may act autonomously, retaining a revocation/halt
      authority.  One receipt authorizes the envelope; autonomy operates
      only inside it and only while unrevoked and unexpired.

3.  Authorization-Context Conventions

   For the autonomy domain the receipt's authorization context MAY
   carry: control_mode (in_the_loop | on_the_loop); and an
   authorization_scope with effect_class, target_set, geofence, and
   window {not_before, not_after}. These are bounds the relying party
   enforces; they do not expand the core receipt's guarantees.

4.  Relying-Party (Enforcement) Rules

   A relying party (Policy Enforcement Point) MUST act only on a valid
   receipt and MUST fail closed otherwise.  For on_the_loop, each
   individual action MUST be checked against the authorized envelope
   (effect class, target set, geofence, window) offline; an out-of-
   envelope, expired, or revoked action MUST be refused.  Continuous
   evaluation and revocation apply (the halt authority).  Higher
   assurance regimes (for example a two-person rule) SHOULD use EP-
   QUORUM (m-of-n distinct humans).  Where an action executes, the
   enforcement point SHOULD emit a post-execution receipt bound to the
   authorization (draft-schrock-ep-enforcement-point).

5.  What It Proves, and What It Does Not

   The profile proves the fact, scope, currency, and authority of a
   named human's (or quorum's) authorization of an exact action or
   bounded envelope, offline-verifiable without trusting the operator.
   It does not prove that the human understood the action, was
   uncoerced, or that the action was lawful, wise, or proportional.
   Display-fidelity is a client-conformance concern; comprehension and
   lawfulness are out of band.  Meaningful human control is necessary,
   not sufficient, and EP supplies the necessary, verifiable part.

6.  Security Considerations

   Over-trust is the dominant risk: treating the existence of a receipt
   as proof an autonomous action was legitimate.  A receipt proves
   authorization at a stated scope, currency, and authority -- nothing
   more.  Coercion of an authorized human and authorization without
   comprehension are out of scope of the cryptographic guarantees;
   device-bound user verification and display-fidelity requirements
   raise but do not eliminate them.  Per-component considerations are
   normative in the referenced documents.

7.  IANA Considerations

   This document registers the control_mode values (in_the_loop,
